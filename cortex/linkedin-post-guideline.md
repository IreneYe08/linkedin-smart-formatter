# LinkedIn Post Guideline

Use this document to turn a raw idea into a high-performing LinkedIn post, or to audit/polish an existing draft.

---

## The Core Principle

Every high-performing LinkedIn post follows the same underlying logic:

**"The world already changed. Most people haven't caught up. Here's what to do about it."**

You're not teaching. You're revealing a gap between reality and what people are still doing. The reader should finish your post feeling slightly behind — and then grateful you showed them the way forward.

---

## Structure Template

Follow these 8 blocks in order. Not every post needs all 8, but the sequence should stay the same.

### 1. Hook (1 line)

The first line is the only line that matters. It appears before "...see more" on mobile. It must create enough tension that people tap.

**Three hook types that work:**

| Type | Formula | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Prediction | "[Thing everyone relies on] is disappearing" | "Prediction: Websites are disappearing" |
| Warning | "[Audience] is making a costly mistake" | "Brands in Hong Kong are burning millions on the wrong customer" |
| Contradiction | "[Common belief] is wrong. Here's why" | "The CPA shortage isn't real. The tolerance shortage is." |

**Rules:**
- One sentence. No exceptions.
- No filler words. No "I've been thinking about..."
- Bold or emoji on the hook line is fine — it's the billboard
- Make a claim strong enough that people MUST click to see if you can back it up

**Bad hooks:**
- "Excited to share some thoughts on AI in accounting" (no tension)
- "Here are 5 tips for better marketing" (listicle energy, no stakes)
- "I've been in this industry for 20 years and..." (leading with credentials)


### 2. Stakes (2-3 lines)

Immediately answer: **why should I care right now?** Connect the hook to a consequence the reader feels personally.

**Example (Felix):**
> I see a lot of founders still building websites the same way they did ten years ago. But if they don't adapt, they'll lose customers.

**Example (Cyril):**
> Let that sink in. The numbers are brutal:

**Rules:**
- Make it personal to the reader's world — their job, their revenue, their career
- "If they don't adapt, they'll _____" is a strong formula
- Keep it short. You're building pressure, not explaining yet.


### 3. Evidence (bullet list or short paragraph)

Back up your claim with specifics. Data, company names, real numbers, observable trends. This is where you earn the right to have an opinion.

**Example (Cyril):**
> - 37.8 million mainland Chinese visitors in 2025
> - 76% of ALL Hong Kong arrivals
> - 1 in 4 residents will be mainland-born by 2046
> - 92,000+ Top Talent Pass approvals

**Example (Felix):**
> Midjourney, ChatGPT, Google, ElevenLabs — they all do it. You land and start using the product immediately.

**Rules:**
- Specific numbers > vague claims ("37.8 million" not "a lot of visitors")
- Name real companies, real tools, real people
- 3-5 data points is the sweet spot. Less feels thin, more feels like a report.
- If you don't have hard data, use observable examples ("open any Big 4 job posting right now and look at...")


### 4. The Mistake (list the wrong moves)

Name exactly what people are doing wrong. Be specific enough that readers recognize themselves.

**Example (Cyril):**
> Now look at the average Hong Kong media plan:
> - Meta. Instagram. Google.
> - Cantonese-first creative
> - Local KOLs for a shrinking local audience
> - Xiaohongshu and Douyin? "We're testing it."

**Example (Felix):**
> Landing on a typical SaaS site today means reading walls of text, watching explainer videos, filling out demo request forms. A lot of friction.

**Rules:**
- List the specific wrong actions, not vague criticisms
- The reader should feel a slight sting of recognition — "wait, that's what I'm doing"
- Adding a quoted phrase people actually say makes it more real ("We're testing it." / "Let's wait and see.")
- Don't insult. Describe. Let them draw the conclusion.

## CONTENT GUIDELINES (from Content_Guidelines_Linkedin.md)

### 1. POST STRUCTURE

Every post must contain **TWO** distinct sections:

- **PART 1: THE VALUE** (100-500 words, carousels, diagrams, or video)
- **PART 2: THE CTA** (A "by the way" gear-change to a specific offer)

---

### 2. THE 5 VALUE HOOKS (3 S's & 2 F's)

- **SCARY:** Industry-disrupting trends, news, or technologies.
- **STRANGE:** Unusual, quirky, or counter-intuitive insights.
- **SEXY:** Desirable outcomes, "the prize," or massive growth results.
- **FAMILIAR:** Leveraging famous people or common industry themes.
- **FREE VALUE:** Giving away high-value research or tools for free.

---

### 3. THE CALL TO ACTION (CTA) RULES

- **DISCONNECTED:** The value content does **NOT** need to relate to the CTA.
- **DIRECT LINKS:** Put the link directly in the post body.
- **ENERGY:** Use a "with or without your energy" approach (low-pressure). try not to make people feel that you are providing this value just only to let people click the link.


### 5. The Gap (1-2 lines)

One sentence that crystallizes the disconnect. This is the emotional peak of the post.

**Example (Cyril):**
> The money is walking in from the north. The marketing is still pointed west.

**Example (Felix):**
> People don't want to read about what your product can do. They want to experience it.

**Rules:**
- Parallel structure works well ("[Reality]. [But people are still doing X].")
- This should be quotable. If someone screenshots one line from your post, it should be this one.
- Keep it to 1-2 sentences max. Brevity is power here.


### 6. The Reset (checklist)

Now tell them what to do instead. Be concrete and actionable. This is where readers feel they got value.

**Example (Cyril):**
> The reset is simple:
> - Xiaohongshu and Douyin as CORE, not test budget
> - Simplified Chinese creative, mainland visual codes
> - Micro-creators with mainland reach > local macro-influencers
> - Keep the Hong Kong soul in the product. Modernize the channels.

**Example (Felix):**
> If I were to start a company today:
> I'd think about the single biggest pain point. Then I wouldn't try to sell them a solution — I'd show them one. Let them interact with it. Make them fall in love with it.

**Rules:**
- 3-5 action items. Not 10.
- Each item should be doable, not philosophical
- Frame as what TO do, not just what to stop doing
- "X as CORE, not Y" format is powerful — it shows the priority shift, not just the action


### 7. Credibility Drop (1 sentence, casual)

Reveal why you're qualified to say this. But AFTER you've already earned attention with the argument itself.

**Example (Cyril):**
> After 25 years moving between Hong Kong and the mainland, I will say it plainly:

**Example (Felix):**
> I think one of the early growth factors for Lovable was exactly this. We doubled down on the "instant value" approach.

**Rules:**
- Never lead with credentials. Earn attention first, then reveal authority.
- One sentence is enough. Don't list your resume.
- Frame it as experience, not status ("after 25 years doing X" not "as a CEO")
- If mentioning your own product, make it evidence for the argument — not the point of the post
- If you don't have personal credibility on the topic, skip this block entirely. The argument should stand on its own.


### 8. Closer (1-2 lines)

End with something that makes the reader reflect on their own situation. Two formats work:

**Binary choice (Cyril style):**
> Which side of that line do you want to be on?

**Open question (Felix style):**
> What's your take on this?

**Mic-drop statement:**
> The best websites won't explain what they do. They'll just do it.

**Rules:**
- Don't end with a CTA to your product. End with a thought.
- Questions drive more comments than statements
- Binary questions ("which side?") drive more engagement than open questions ("what do you think?") because they're easier to answer
- A strong final statement + a question is the strongest combo

## Formatting Rules

### Do:
- One idea per paragraph. Often one SENTENCE per paragraph.
- Use line breaks aggressively. White space is your friend on mobile.
- Bold or unicode bold for the hook line
- Bullet lists for evidence and action items
- Keep total length between 150-250 words (the sweet spot for engagement without losing people)

### Don't:
- No walls of text. If a paragraph is more than 3 lines on mobile, break it.
- No hashtags in the body. If you use them, stack them at the very end.
- No "I'm excited to announce..." or "Thrilled to share..." openers. Ever.
- No corporate jargon ("leverage," "synergies," "holistic approach")
- No more than 3-4 emoji per post unless your brand voice is emoji-heavy (like Cyril's). If unsure, use zero.

## Tone Spectrum

Choose your lane based on the topic and your brand:

| Tone | When to use | Sounds like |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Calm analytical | Explaining a trend, sharing a framework | "Here's what I'm seeing and what it means" |
| Urgent provocateur | Industry is making a clear mistake | "Wake up. The numbers don't lie." |
| Personal storyteller | Sharing a lesson from experience | "I learned this the hard way last year" |
| Dry/witty observer | Pointing out absurdity | "We spent $40k on a consulting deck that said what the intern told us for free" |

You can mix tones within a post, but the hook should set the dominant tone.

## Audit Checklist

Use this to review a draft before posting. Score each item pass/fail.

### Hook
- [ ] First line creates tension, curiosity, or a strong claim
- [ ] Would I click "see more" if I saw this in my feed?
- [ ] No filler words, no soft opening

### Structure
- [ ] One core idea — can I summarize the post in one sentence?
- [ ] Evidence is specific (names, numbers, examples — not vibes)
- [ ] The "mistake" section is concrete enough that readers recognize themselves
- [ ] The "reset" gives actionable steps, not just philosophy
- [ ] Credibility comes AFTER the argument, not before

### Formatting
- [ ] Every paragraph is 1-3 lines max
- [ ] Scannable on mobile (bullet lists, line breaks, bold)
- [ ] Total length is 150-250 words
- [ ] No corporate jargon

### Engagement
- [ ] Ends with a question or statement that forces self-reflection
- [ ] The post invites disagreement (not everyone will agree = more comments)
- [ ] If mentioning own product, it's evidence not pitch (max 1 mention)

### Final Gut Check
- [ ] Would I engage with this post if someone else wrote it?
- [ ] Does it make the reader feel something (behind, curious, validated, challenged)?
- [ ] Is there one line someone might screenshot and share?

## Quick-Start: Turning a Raw Idea Into a Post

When you have a rough idea, run through these 4 questions:

1. **What changed?** (the shift most people haven't noticed)
2. **Who's still doing it the old way?** (the mistake — be specific)
3. **What should they do instead?** (the reset — be actionable)
4. **Why should they listen to me?** (your proof — experience, data, or example)

Write your answers. Then arrange them in the 8-block structure above. The post practically writes itself.
